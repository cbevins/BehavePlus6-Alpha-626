Lightning Strike Type

Lightning strike type is the type of lightning discharge (polarity). It can be

  • negative (-)
  • positive (+)
  • unknown (?)

I/O Module If Notes
Input IGNITE If Probability of Ignition from Lightning is selected as an output variable.  
Output None    

The characteristic of lightning responsible for ignition of ground fuel is the presence and duration of a continuing current, which varies by Lightning Strike Type. The presence or duration of a continuing current is not detected by the current operational lightning detection network.

  • Positive strikes
    • About 10 to 25% of all cloud-to-ground flashes are positive. Their physical origins are poorly understood. About 90 percent of all positive cloud-to-ground flashes have a continuing current, but the duration of the continuing current is shorter than that for negative flashes (see figure below). Positive flashes are usually a single stroke, and typically occur in the dissipating stage of a thunderstorm. The naked eye cannot discern a positive from negative flash.
  • Negative strikes
    • About 75 to 90% of all cloud-to-ground flashes are negative; the majority are multi-stroke. Only about 20% of negative flashes have a continuing current, but those that do have longer duration than positive flashes (see figure below).
  • Strikes of unknown polarity
    • Since it is difficult to know or predict whether a strike has positive or negative polarity an estimate is made for strikes of unknown polarity by weighting the positive and negative strike probabilities by their relative frequency of occurrence.

Latham and Schlieter (1989) noted that 72.3% of strikes have negative polarity and 27.7% have positive polarity. The weighted probability of a strike with unknown polarity or continuing current starting a fire is therefore estimated as follows.

  • Punknown = 0.723 × 0.2 × Pnegative + 0.277 × 0.9 × Ppositive
