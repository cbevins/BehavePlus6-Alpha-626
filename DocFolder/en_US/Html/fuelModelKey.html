Fuel Model Key

The original 13 standard fire behavior fuel models are described in Anderson (1982). These fuel models were used by Albini (1976b) to develop the nomograms published in his paper Estimating wildfire behavior and effects. The 40 fuel models are described by Scott and Burgan (2005).

General Considerations when Selecting a Fuel Model

  1. Determine the general vegetation type, i.e., grass, brush, timber litter, or slash.
  2. Estimate which stratum of surface fuel is most likely to carry the spreading fire.
    • For instance, the fire may be in a timbered area, but the timber is relatively open and the dead grass, not needle litter, would carry the fire. In this case, fuel model 2, which is not listed as a timber model, should be considered.
    • In the same area if the grass is sparse and there is no wind or slope, the needle litter would be the stratum carrying the fire and fuel model 9 may be a better choice.
  3. Note the general depth and compactness of the fuel. This information will be needed when using the fuel model key. These are very important considerations, particularly in the grass and timber types.
  4. Determine which fuel classes are present and estimate their influence on fire behavior.
    • For instance, green fuel may be present. Will it play a significant role in fire behavior?
    • Large fuels may be present, but are they sound or decaying and breaking up?
    • Do they have limbs and twigs attached or are they bare cylinders?
    • You must look for fine fuels and choose a model that represents their depth, compactness, and to some extent, the amount of live fuel and its contribution to fire.
    • Do not be restricted by the fuel model name or category.
  5. Using these observations, proceed through the fuel model key and the descriptions provided by Rothermel (1983) or Scott and Burgan (2005) to select a model.

Key to the 13 Original Fire Behavior Fuel Models (Rothermel 1983)

I. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS GRASS.
Expected rate of spread is moderate to high, with low to moderate intensity (flame length).
A. Grass is fine structured, generally below knee level, and cured or primarily dead. Grass is essentially continuous.
Consider fuel model 1.
B. Grass is coarse structured, above knee level (averaging about 3 feet (0.9 meters)), and is difficult to walk through.
Consider fuel model 3.
C. Grass is usually under an open timber or brush overstory. Litter from the overstory is involved, but grass carries the fire. Expected spread rate is slower than fuel model 1 and intensity is less than fuel model 3.
Consider fuel model 2.
 
II. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS BRUSH OR LITTER BENEATH BRUSH.
Expected rate of spread and fireline intensity (flame length) is moderate to high.
A. Vegetative type is southern rough or low pocosin. Brush is generally 2 to 4 feet (0.6 to 1.2 meters) high.
Consider fuel model 7.
B. Live fuels are absent or sparse. Brush height averages 2 to 4 feet (0.6 to 1.2 meters). Brush requires moderate winds to carry the fire.
Consider fuel model 6.
C. Live fuel moisture may have a significant effect on fire behavior.
1. Brush is about 2 feet (0.6 meters) high with light loading of brush litter underneath. Litter may carry the fire, especially at low wind speed.
Consider fuel model 5.
2. Brush is head high (6 feet, 1.8 meters) with a heavy loading of dead woody fuel. Very intense fire with high spread rates are expected.
Consider fuel model 4.
3. Vegetative type is high pocosin. Consider fuel model 4.
 
III. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS LITTER BENEATH A TIMBER STAND.
Spread rate is low to moderate; fireline intensity (flame length) may be low to high.
A. Surface fuels are mostly foliage litter. Large fuels are scattered and lie on the foliage litter; i.e., large fuels are not supported above the litter by their branches. Green fuels are scattered enough to be insignificant to fire behavior.
1. Dead foliage is tightly compacted, short needle (2 inches (5 cm) or less) conifer litter or hardwood litter.
Consider fuel model 8.
2. Dead foliage litter is loosely compacted long needle pine or hardwoods.
Consider fuel model 9.
B. There is a significant amount of larger fuel. Larger fuel has attached branches and twigs, or has rotted enough that it is splintered and broken. The larger fuels are fairly well distributed over the area. Some green fuel may be present. The overall depth of the fuel is probably below the knees, but some fuel may be higher.
Consider fuel model 10.
 
IV. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS LOGGING SLASH.
Spread rate is low to high; fireline intensity (flame length) is low to very high.
A. Slash is aged and overgrown.
1. Slash is from hardwood trees. leaves have fallen and cured. Considerable vegetation (tall weeds) has grown in amid the slash and has cured or dried out.
Consider fuel model 6.
2. Slash is from conifers. Needles have fallen and considerable vegetation (tall weeds and some shrubs) has overgrown the slash.
Consider fuel model 10.
B. Slash is fresh (0-3 years or so) and not overly compacted.
1. Slash is not continuous. Needle litter or small amounts of grass or shrubs must be present to help carry the fire, but the primary carrier is still slash. Live fuels are absent or do not play a significant role in fire behavior. The slash depth is about 1 foot (0.3 meters).
Consider fuel model 11.
2. Slash generally covers the ground (heavier loadings than fuel model 11), though there may be some bare spots or areas of light coverage. Average slash depth is about 2 feet (0.6 meters). Slash is not excessively compacted. Approximately one-half of the needles may still be on the branches but are not red. Live fuels are absent, or are not expected to affect fire behavior.
Consider fuel model 12.
3. Slash is continuous or nearly so (heavier loadings than fuel model 12). Slash is not excessively compacted and has an average depth of 3 feet (0.9 meters). Approximately one-half of the needles are still on the branches and are red, OR all the needles are on the branches but they are green. Live fuels are not expected to influence fire behavior.
Consider fuel model 13.
4. Same as previous, EXCEPT all the needles are attached and are red.
Consider fuel model 4.

Key to the 40 Additional Fire Behavior Fuel Models (Scott and Burgan 2005)


I. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS NEARLY PURE GRASS AND/OR FORB (GRASS).
A. Arid to semiarid climate (rainfall deficient in summer). Extinction moisture content is 15 percent.
i. GR1/101 Grass is short, patchy, and possibly heavily grazed. Spread rate moderate; flame length low.
ii. GR2/102 Moderately coarse continuous grass, average depth about 1 foot (0.3 meters). Spread rate high; flame length moderate.
iii. GR4/104 Moderately coarse continuous grass, average depth about 2 feet (0.6 meters). Spread rate very high; flame length high.
iv. GR7/107 Moderately coarse continuous grass, average depth about 3 feet (0.9 meters). Spread rate very high; flame length very high.
B. Subhumid to humid climate (rainfall adequate in all seasons). Extinction moisture content is 30 to 40 percent.
i. GR1/101 Grass is short, patchy, and possibly heavily grazed. Spread rate moderate; flame length low.
ii. GR3/103 Very coarse grass, average depth about 2 feet (0.6 meters). Spread rate high; flame length moderate.
iii. GR5/105 Dense, coarse grass, average depth about 1 to 2 feet (0.3 to 0.6 meters). Spread rate very high; flame length high.
iv. GR6/106 Dryland grass about 1 to 2 feet (0.3 to 0.6 meters) tall. Spread rate very high; flame length very high.
v. GR8/108 Heavy, coarse, continuous grass 3 to 5 feet (0.9 to 1.5 meters) tall. Spread rate very high; flame length very high.
vi. GR9/109 Very heavy, coarse, continuous grass 5 to 8 feet (1.5 to 2.4 meters) tall. Spread rate extreme; flame length extreme.
 
II. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS MIXTURE OF GRASS AND SHRUB,
UP TO ABOUT 50% SHRUB COVERAGE (GRASS-SHRUB).
A. Arid to semiarid climate (rainfall deficient in summer). Extinction moisture content is 15 percent.
i. GS1/121 Shrubs are about 1 foot (0.3 meters) high, low grass load. Spread rate moderate; flame length low.
ii. GS2/122 Shrubs are 1 to 3 feet (0.3 to 0.9 meters) high, moderate grass load. Spread rate high; flame length moderate.
B. Subhumid to humid climate (rainfall adequate in all seasons). Extinction moisture content is 30 to 40 percent.
i. GS3/123 Moderate grass/shrub load, average grass/shrub depth less than 2 feet (0.6 meters). Spread rate high; flame length moderate.
ii. GS4/124 Heavy grass/shrub load, depth greater than 2 feet (0.6 meters). Spread rate high; flame length very high.
 
III. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS SHRUB; SHRUBS COVER AT LEAST 50% OF THE SITE;
GRASS SPARSE TO NONEXISTENT (SHRUB).
A. Arid to semiarid climate (rainfall deficient in summer). Extinction moisture content is 15 percent.
i. SH1/141 Low shrub fuel load, fuel bed depth about 1 foot (0.3 meters); some grass may be present. Spread rate very low; flame length very low.
ii. SH2/142 Moderate fuel load (higher than SH1), depth about 1 foot (0.3 meters), no grass fuel present. Spread rate low; flame length low.
iii. SH5/145 Heavy shrub load, depth 4 to 6 feet (1.2 to 1.8 meters). Spread rate very high; flame length very high.
iv. SH7/147 Very heavy shrub load, depth 4 to 6 feet (1.2 to 1.8 meters). Spread rate lower than SH5, but flame length similar. Spread rate high; flame length very high.
B. Subhumid to humid climate (rainfall adequate in all seasons). Extinction moisture content is 30 to 40 percent.
i. SH3/143 Moderate shrub load, possibly with pine overstory or herbaceous fuel, fuel bed depth 2 to 3 feet (0.3 to 0.6 meters). Spread rate low; flame length low.
ii. SH4/144 Low to moderate shrub and litter load, possibly with pine overstory, fuel bed depth about 3 feet (0.6 meters). Spread rate high; flame length moderate.
iii. SH6/146 Dense shrubs, little or no herb fuel, depth about 2 feet (0.3 meters). Spread rate high; flame length high.
iv. SH8/148 Dense shrubs, little or no herb fuel, depth about 3 feet (0.6 meters). Spread rates high; flame length high.
v. SH9/149 Dense, finely branched shrubs with significant fine dead fuel, about 4 to 6 feet tall (1.2 to 1.8 meters); some herbaceous fuel may be present. Spread rate high, flame length very high.
 
IV. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS GRASS OR SHRUBS MIXED WITH LITTER FROM FOREST CANOPY (TIMBER-UNDERSTORY).
A. Semiarid to subhumid climate. Extinction moisture content is 20 percent.
i. TU1/161 Fuel bed is low load of grass and/or shrub with litter. Spread rate low; flame length low.
ii. TU4/164 Fuel bed is short conifer trees with grass or moss understory. Spread rate moderate; flame length moderate.
iii. TU5/165 Fuel bed is high load conifer litter with shrub understory. Spread rate moderate; flame length moderate.
B. Humid climate. Extinction moisture content is 30 percent.
i. TU2/162 Fuel bed is moderate litter load with shrub component. Spread rate moderate; flame length low.
ii. TU3/163 Fuel bed is moderate litter load with grass and shrub components. Spread rate high; flame length moderate.
 
V. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS DEAD AND DOWN WOODY FUEL (LITTER) BENEATH A FOREST CANOPY (TIMBER LITTER).
A. Fuel bed is recently burned but able to carry wildland fire.
i. TL1/181 Light to moderate load, fuels 1 to 2 inches (2.5 to 5 cm) deep. Spread rate very low; flame length very low.
B. Fuel bed not recently burned.
i. Fuel bed composed of broadleaf (hardwood) litter.
1. TL2/182 Low load, compact. Spread rate very low; flame length very low.
2. TL6/186 Moderate load, less compact. Spread rate moderate; flame length low.
3. TL9/189 Very high load, fluffy. Spread rate moderate; flame length moderate.
ii. Fuel bed composed of long-needle pine litter.
1. TL8/188 Moderate load and compactness may include small amount of herbaceous load. Spread rate moderate; flame length low.
iii. Fuel bed not composed of broadleaf or long-needle pine litter.
1. Fuel bed includes both fine and coarse fuels.
a. TL4/184 Moderate load, includes small diameter downed logs. Spread rate low; flame length low.
b. TL7/187 Heavy load, includes larger diameter downed logs. Spread rate low; flame length low.
2. Fuel bed does not include coarse fuels.
a. TL3/183 Moderate load conifer litter. Spread rate very low; flame length low.
b. TL5/185 High load conifer litter; light slash or mortality fuel. Spread rate low; flame length low.
c. TL9/189 Very high load broadleaf litter; heavy needle-drape in otherwise sparse shrub layer. Spread rate moderate; flame length moderate.
 
VI. PRIMARY CARRIER OF THE FIRE IS ACTIVITY FUEL (SLASH) OR DEBRIS FROM WIND DAMAGE (BLOWDOWN) (SLASH-BLOWDOWN).
A. Fuel bed is activity fuel.
i. SB1/201 Fine fuel load is 10 to 20 tons/acre (22 to 45 tonne/ha), weighted toward fuels 1 to 3 inches (2.54 to 7.62 cm) diameter class, depth is less than 1 foot (0.3 meters). Spread rate moderate; flame length low.
ii. SB2/202 Fine fuel load is 7 to 12 tons/acre (16 to 27 tonne/ha), evenly distributed across 0 to 0.25 inch (0 to 0.64 cm), 0.25 to 1 inch (0.64 to 2.5 cm), and 1 to 3 inch (2.5 to 7.6 cm) diameter classes, depth is about 1 foot (0.3 meters). Spread rate moderate; flame length moderate.
iii. SB3/203 Fine fuel load is 7 to 12 tons/acre (16 to 27 tonne/ha), weighted toward 0 to 0.25 inch (0 to 0.64 cm) diameter class, depth is more than 1 foot (0.3 meters). Spread rate high; flame length high.
B. Fuel bed is blowdown.
i. SB2/202 Blowdown is scattered, with many trees still standing. Spread rate moderate; flame length moderate.
ii. SB3/203 Blowdown is moderate, trees compacted to near the ground. Spread rate high; flame length high.
iii. SB4/204 Blowdown is total, fuel bed not compacted, foliage still attached. Spread rate very high; flame length very high.
